§ 01 s-Block · Group 1 — Alkali Metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs) ~35 RXNs
WITH DIOXYGEN / O₂
TREND Li → oxide only | Na → peroxide (Na₂O₂) | K, Rb, Cs → superoxide (MO₂). Reactivity with O₂ increases down the group.
R1
4Li + O₂ → 2Li₂O limited O₂, room temp
ANOMALOUS Li forms normal oxide only — too small to accommodate O₂²⁻ or O₂⁻ in lattice
SYNTH
R2
2Na + O₂ → Na₂O₂ excess O₂
JEE FREQ Na burns in excess O₂ to give sodium peroxide (not Na₂O). Na₂O₂ is pale yellow, diamagnetic
SYNTH
R3
4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O limited O₂, careful burning
Sodium oxide — white solid; obtained only under very controlled conditions
SYNTH
R4
K + O₂ → KO₂ excess O₂
SUPEROXIDE KO₂ is paramagnetic (O₂⁻ has one unpaired e⁻). Same for RbO₂, CsO₂
SYNTH
R5
Rb + O₂ → RbO₂  |  Cs + O₂ → CsO₂
Both form superoxides as primary product with excess O₂
SYNTH
WITH WATER / H₂O
DANGER Reactivity increases dramatically: Li (slow, controlled) → Na (vigorous) → K (bursts into flame) → Rb, Cs (explosive). All reactions exothermic; H₂ gas evolved.
R6
2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂↑
Slowest reaction among alkali metals; does not catch fire. LiOH has lowest solubility among group 1 hydroxides
REDOX
R7
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑
CLASSIC Vigorous; Na floats and moves. H₂ may ignite. NaOH solution is strongly alkaline
REDOX
R8
2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂↑ ← catches fire (lilac flame)
H₂ ignites spontaneously. K burns with characteristic lilac/violet flame
REDOX
R9
2Rb/Cs + 2H₂O → 2Rb/CsOH + H₂↑ ← EXPLOSIVE
EXTREME Violent/explosive reactions. Cs reacts most vigorously of all stable alkali metals
REDOX
WITH N₂ · H₂ · LIQUID NH₃
R10
6Li + N₂ → 2Li₃N room temp
ANOMALOUS ONLY alkali metal to react with N₂ directly at room temperature — due to small ionic radius and high charge density, similar to Mg. Li₃N is a good ionic conductor
SYNTH
R11
Li₃N + 3H₂O → 3LiOH + NH₃↑
Hydrolysis of lithium nitride gives ammonia — used to detect Li₃N
ACID-B
R12
2Na + H₂ → 2NaH 300 °C, pressure
Sodium hydride — ionic hydride; strong base and reducing agent. NaH + H₂O → NaOH + H₂↑
SYNTH
R13
2Li + H₂ → 2LiH 300–500 °C
Most stable alkali metal hydride. LiH is used in nuclear fusion as H source
SYNTH
R14
2Na + 2NH₃(liq) → 2NaNH₂ + H₂↑ Fe³⁺ catalyst, slow
SODAMIDE NaNH₂ (sodium amide) is a very strong base (pKₐ of NH₃ ≈ 38). Used in organic synthesis
REDOX
R15
Na + NH₃(liq) → Na⁺(NH₃) + e⁻(NH₃) dilute, blue
AMMONIATED e⁻ Dilute solution of Na in liquid NH₃ is BLUE (paramagnetic, good conductor). Concentrated solution is bronze-coloured
REDOX
WITH HALOGENS
R16
2M + X₂ → 2MX M = Li,Na,K,Rb,Cs; X = F,Cl,Br,I
All alkali metals react vigorously with all halogens to form ionic halides MX
SYNTH
R17
2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl vigorous, bright yellow flame
JEE FREQ Reaction is highly exothermic; Na catches fire in Cl₂. Product is white crystalline salt
REDOX
THERMAL DECOMPOSITION — NITRATES, CARBONATES, HYDROXIDES
TREND Stability of carbonates and hydroxides increases down the group (smaller cation = more polarising = less stable). Li is anomalous — behaves like Mg (diagonal relationship).
R18
2NaNO₃ → 2NaNO₂ + O₂↑ ~380 °C
CONTRAST Na/K/Rb/Cs nitrates give NITRITE + O₂ on heating — unlike Li and Group 2 which give oxide + NO₂ + O₂
DECOMP
R19
4LiNO₃ → 2Li₂O + 4NO₂↑ + O₂↑ Δ
ANOMALOUS Li nitrate decomposes like Mg(NO₃)₂ (gives oxide, not nitrite). Diagonal relationship with Mg
DECOMP
R20
Li₂CO₃ → Li₂O + CO₂↑ Δ, ~700 °C
ANOMALOUS ONLY Li₂CO₃ decomposes among Group 1 carbonates. Na₂CO₃, K₂CO₃ etc. are thermally stable up to melting point
DECOMP
R21
2LiOH → Li₂O + H₂O Δ
ANOMALOUS LiOH is the least stable Group 1 hydroxide and decomposes on heating. NaOH, KOH do NOT decompose easily
DECOMP
REACTIONS OF NaOH
R22
NaOH + CO₂(excess) → NaHCO₃
Excess CO₂ → bicarbonate. Limited CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O
ACID-B
R23
2NaOH + SO₂ → Na₂SO₃ + H₂O  |  NaOH + SO₂(excess) → NaHSO₃
Analogous to CO₂ reactions. Excess SO₂ gives bisulphite
ACID-B
R24
2NaOH + Cl₂ → NaCl + NaOCl + H₂O cold, dilute
JEE FREQ Cold/dilute NaOH + Cl₂ → hypochlorite (NaOCl). Hot/conc → NaCl + NaClO₃ (chlorate) + H₂O
DISP
R25
6NaOH + 3Cl₂ → NaClO₃ + 5NaCl + 3H₂O hot, conc
HOT vs COLD Hot NaOH gives chlorate. This distinction is frequently tested
DISP
R26
2Al + 2NaOH + 2H₂O → 2NaAlO₂ + 3H₂↑
Al is amphoteric; dissolves in both acids and bases. NaAlO₂ = sodium aluminate
REDOX
Na₂O₂ — SODIUM PEROXIDE REACTIONS
STRUCTURE Na₂O₂ contains the peroxide ion O₂²⁻ (O—O single bond). It is a strong oxidising agent and acts as an O₂ source in enclosed spaces.
R27
2Na₂O₂ + 2H₂O → 4NaOH + O₂↑ [H₂O₂ formed first, then decomposes]
2-STEP Na₂O₂ + H₂O → NaOH + H₂O₂ (step 1); 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (step 2). H₂O₂ decomposes due to basic medium
REDOX
R28
Na₂O₂ + H₂SO₄(dil) → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O₂
PREP Laboratory preparation of H₂O₂. Dilute acid stabilises H₂O₂ — prevents decomposition in acidic medium
ACID-B
R29
2Na₂O₂ + 2CO₂ → 2Na₂CO₃ + O₂↑
SUBMARINE Na₂O₂ absorbs exhaled CO₂ and releases O₂ — used in submarines and breathing masks. Self-contained O₂ supply
REDOX
R30
Na₂O₂ + SO₂ → Na₂SO₄
Na₂O₂ oxidises SO₂ to SO₄²⁻. Analogous to reaction with CO₂ but no O₂ released
REDOX
R31
Na₂O₂ + CO → Na₂CO₃
Na₂O₂ oxidises CO to CO₂, then CO₂ reacts with excess Na₂O₂ → Na₂CO₃
REDOX
KO₂ — POTASSIUM SUPEROXIDE REACTIONS
R32
4KO₂ + 2H₂O → 4KOH + 3O₂↑
MORE O₂ KO₂ releases more O₂ per mole than Na₂O₂. KO₂ is preferred in modern breathing apparatus
REDOX
R33
4KO₂ + 2CO₂ → 2K₂CO₃ + 3O₂↑
BREATHING MASK KO₂ removes CO₂ and replenishes O₂ simultaneously. Used in fire-fighting and space suits
REDOX
SOLVAY PROCESS — Na₂CO₃ PRODUCTION & RELATED REACTIONS
INDUSTRIAL Solvay process: NaCl (brine) + NH₃ + CO₂ → NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃. Most important industrial process for alkali carbonates.
R34
NaCl + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃↓ + NH₄Cl
STEP 1 NaHCO₃ precipitates because it is least soluble. NH₄Cl stays in solution
INDUS
R35
2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂↑ Δ, calcination
STEP 2 Heating NaHCO₃ gives washing soda (Na₂CO₃). CO₂ recycled back to Step 1
INDUS
R36
2NH₄Cl + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCl₂ + 2NH₃↑ + 2H₂O Δ
NH₃ RECOVERY NH₃ is recovered and recycled. CaCl₂ is byproduct. Solvay process uses limestone: CaCO₃ → CaO → Ca(OH)₂
INDUS
R37
Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ → 2NaHCO₃
Reverse reaction — Na₂CO₃ (washing soda) reacts with CO₂/H₂O to give baking soda NaHCO₃
ACID-B
R38
2NaCl + 2H₂O →electrolysis 2NaOH + Cl₂↑ + H₂↑
CHLOR-ALKALI At cathode: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻. At anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻. All three products are industrially important
INDUS
§ 02 s-Block · Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba) ~38 RXNs
WITH WATER AND STEAM
TREND Be — no reaction with water. Mg — reacts only with steam. Ca/Sr/Ba — react with cold water. Reactivity increases down group.
R39
Be + H₂O → No Reaction passivated by BeO layer
PASSIVE Be is protected by a thin, adherent BeO film — doesn't react with water or steam under normal conditions
REDOX
R40
Mg + H₂O(steam) → MgO + H₂↑ red heat
STEAM ONLY Mg reacts with steam but NOT cold water (slow surface oxide passivation). Brilliant white flame if Mg burns in steam
REDOX
R41
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑ cold water, steady
Ca reacts steadily with cold water. Ca(OH)₂ is slightly soluble (lime water). Sr and Ba react more vigorously
REDOX
R42
Ba + 2H₂O → Ba(OH)₂ + H₂↑ vigorous
Ba(OH)₂ is the most soluble Group 2 hydroxide. Ba reacts most vigorously with water among group 2
REDOX
WITH DIOXYGEN / O₂
R43
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO brilliant white flame
JEE FREQ Mg also reacts with N₂ simultaneously: 3Mg + N₂ → Mg₃N₂. Real product of Mg burning in air is mixture of MgO and Mg₃N₂
REDOX
R44
2Ca + O₂ → 2CaO
All Group 2 metals burn in O₂ to form normal oxides MO (except Ba)
REDOX
R45
2Ba + O₂(excess) → 2BaO₂ at ~600 °C
UNIQUE Ba forms barium peroxide (BaO₂) — only Group 2 metal to do so in excess O₂. BaO₂ decomposes back to BaO + O₂ above 800°C
REDOX
WITH N₂ AND H₂
R46
3Mg + N₂ → Mg₃N₂ when Mg burns in air
JEE FREQ Mg₃N₂ is yellow-green. Hydrolysis: Mg₃N₂ + 6H₂O → 3Mg(OH)₂ + 2NH₃. Explains why burning Mg has ammonia smell
SYNTH
R47
3Ca + N₂ → Ca₃N₂ Δ
All alkaline earth metals form nitrides M₃N₂ when heated in N₂
SYNTH
R48
Mg₃N₂ + 6H₂O → 3Mg(OH)₂ + 2NH₃↑
Nitride hydrolysis always gives the metal hydroxide + ammonia. Useful in detecting nitrides
ACID-B
R49
Ca + H₂ → CaH₂ Δ  |  CaH₂ + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + 2H₂↑
CaH₂ (calcium hydride) is a portable H₂ source — used in field hydrogen generation for balloons
SYNTH
BERYLLIUM — ANOMALOUS BEHAVIOUR (Diagonal Relationship with Al)
ANOMALOUS Be resembles Al (diagonal relationship): both are amphoteric, form covalent compounds, form alums, BeF₂·Al₂O₃ analogy, BeO ↔ Al₂O₃ amphoteric, BeCl₂ Lewis acid like AlCl₃.
R50
Be + 2NaOH(conc) → Na₂BeO₂ + H₂↑
AMPHOTERIC Be dissolves in conc NaOH — ONLY Group 2 metal to do so. Analogous to Al + NaOH. Na₂BeO₂ = sodium beryllate
REDOX
R51
BeO + 2NaOH → Na₂BeO₂ + H₂O
BeO is amphoteric like Al₂O₃. Also reacts with HCl: BeO + 2HCl → BeCl₂ + H₂O
ACID-B
R52
BeCl₂ → (BeCl₂)₂ dimerises (solid state); or BeCl₂ + 2Cl⁻ → [BeCl₄]²⁻
LEWIS ACID BeCl₂ has empty p orbital on Be (sp hybridised, 2 bond pairs). Acts as Lewis acid like AlCl₃. Forms tetrahedral [BeCl₄]²⁻ with excess Cl⁻
ACID-B
R53
BeCl₂ + 2NaCl → Na₂[BeCl₄]
Tetrachloroberyllate — Be achieves octet via dative bonds from Cl⁻. Be in sp³ in this complex (tetrahedral)
ACID-B
MAGNESIUM — SPECIAL REACTIONS
R54
2Mg + CO₂ → 2MgO + C burning Mg in CO₂
CANNOT EXTINGUISH Burning Mg cannot be doused with CO₂ — it continues burning. C (soot) deposited. Also: 3Mg + N₂ → Mg₃N₂ in air
REDOX
R55
R55
MgCl₂·6H₂O → Mg(OH)Cl + 5H₂O + HCl Δ
HYDROLYSIS MgCl₂·6H₂O cannot give anhydrous MgCl₂ on heating — it hydrolyses. To get anhydrous MgCl₂, dehydrate in HCl atmosphere
DECOMP
CALCIUM — KEY REACTIONS AND COMPOUNDS
R56
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂↑ ~840 °C, endothermic
INDUSTRIAL Calcination of limestone. CaO = quicklime. Basis of cement and steel manufacture. Equilibrium: high T shifts decomposition right
INDUS
R57
CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + heat highly exothermic
SLAKING CaO (quicklime) + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime). Enough heat generated to cause steam; used in self-heating cans
ACID-B
R58
Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O limewater turns milky
JEE CLASSIC Confirmatory test for CO₂. Limewater turns milky → turbid (CaCO₃ ppt). Excess CO₂ clears the turbidity
ACID-B
R59
CaCO₃↓ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca(HCO₃)₂ excess CO₂
EXPLAINS Why milkiness disappears with excess CO₂. Ca(HCO₃)₂ = soluble. This is TEMPORARY HARDNESS of water
ACID-B
R60
Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O dry slaked lime + Cl₂, 40 °C
BLEACHING POWDER CaOCl₂ = Ca(OCl)Cl. Contains OCl⁻ as active species. Not a true mixture — it's a mixed salt of hypochlorous and hydrochloric acids
SYNTH
R61
CaOCl₂ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + Cl₂↑ + H₂O
BLEACHING ACTION Bleaching powder + acid liberates Cl₂. Also: CaOCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O → CaCO₃ + Cl₂ + H₂O (releases Cl₂ in air over time)
ACID-B
R62
CaOCl₂ + H₂SO₄(dil) → CaSO₄ + Cl₂↑ + H₂O
Dilute H₂SO₄ displaces Cl₂ from bleaching powder — lab method for Cl₂ generation
ACID-B
R63
2(CaSO₄·2H₂O) → (CaSO₄)₂·H₂O + 3H₂O ~120 °C
PLASTER OF PARIS Gypsum (2H₂O) → Plaster of Paris (½H₂O). Setting: (CaSO₄)₂·H₂O + 3H₂O → 2(CaSO₄·2H₂O). Interlocking crystals cause hardening
DECOMP
R64
CaO + CO₂ → CaCO₃ low temp
Reverse of calcination — occurs at lower temperature. Equilibrium position determines which direction proceeds
ACID-B
R65
CaO + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O  |  CaO + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O
CaO is a basic oxide — neutralises acids. Used as flue gas scrubber (CaO + SO₂ → CaSO₃)
ACID-B
R66
Ca(OH)₂ + SO₂ → CaSO₃ + H₂O
DESULPHURISATION Lime scrubbing — removes SO₂ from industrial emissions. CaSO₃ can be oxidised to CaSO₄ (gypsum)
INDUS
BARIUM — KEY REACTIONS
R67
BaO₂ + H₂SO₄(dil) → BaSO₄↓ + H₂O₂
CLASSIC PREP Historically important preparation of H₂O₂ (Thénard's method). BaSO₄ precipitates, driving reaction forward. H₂O₂ obtained in filtrate
ACID-B
R68
2BaO₂ → 2BaO + O₂ >800 °C
BaO₂ decomposes at high temperature — basis of Brin's process for O₂ (reversible: 2BaO + O₂ ⇌ 2BaO₂)
DECOMP
R69
Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄↓ white ppt, insoluble in dil. HCl
JEE SALT ANALYSIS Confirmatory test for Ba²⁺ and SO₄²⁻. BaSO₄ is insoluble in dilute acids — distinguishes from BaCO₃, BaSO₃ (which dissolve in HCl)
ACID-B
THERMAL DECOMPOSITION — GROUP 2 NITRATES, CARBONATES, SULPHATES
TREND Stability increases DOWN the group. BaCO₃ most stable; MgCO₃ least stable. Polarising power of cation decreases down group (larger cation = less charge density = more stable anion).
R70
MgCO₃ → MgO + CO₂↑ ~350 °C (lowest temp)
MgCO₃ decomposes at lowest temperature — Mg²⁺ has highest charge density, most polarising, least stable carbonate
DECOMP
R71
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂↑ ~840 °C
Standard calcination; industrially crucial. BaCO₃ decomposes at ~1350°C (most stable). Stability: MgCO₃ ≪ CaCO₃ < SrCO₃ < BaCO₃
DECOMP
R72
2Mg(NO₃)₂ → 2MgO + 4NO₂↑ + O₂↑ Δ
ALL GROUP 2 ALL Group 2 nitrates and Li nitrate give oxide + NO₂ + O₂. Contrasts with Na/K/Rb/Cs nitrates → nitrite + O₂
DECOMP
R73
2M(NO₃)₂ → 2MO + 4NO₂↑ + O₂↑ M = Ca, Sr, Ba
General reaction for all Group 2 metal nitrates. Higher temperatures needed going down the group
DECOMP
R74
MgSO₄ → MgO + SO₃↑ very high T >1000°C
Sulphates are thermally very stable. BaSO₄ does NOT decompose at any laboratory temperature — used as a standard for thermal stability tests
DECOMP
§ 03 General Inorganic Chemistry — Industrial & Key Processes ~25 RXNs
HABER PROCESS — AMMONIA SYNTHESIS
R75
N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ 450–500 °C, 200 atm, Fe/Al₂O₃/K₂O ΔH = −92 kJ/mol
INDUSTRIAL Exothermic + decrease in moles → favour low T, high P (Le Chatelier). 450°C is a compromise between yield and rate. Promoters: K₂O (electronic), Al₂O₃ (structural)
INDUS
OSTWALD PROCESS — NITRIC ACID (HNO₃)
R76
4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O Pt-Rh gauze, 800–900 °C ΔH = −907 kJ
STEP 1 — CATALYTIC OXIDATION NH₃ oxidised to NO. Catalyst: Pt-Rh alloy gauze. Reaction is highly exothermic and fast
INDUS
R77
2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂ uncatalysed, cooling
STEP 2 Gas cooled; NO oxidised spontaneously to NO₂. Rate ∝ [NO]²[O₂] — third-order reaction. Rate increases at lower T (unusual)
REDOX
R78
3NO₂ + H₂O → 2HNO₃ + NO↑
STEP 3 NO₂ absorbed in water. NO produced is recycled back. Final product ~68% HNO₃; concentrated further by distillation
INDUS
R79
4NO₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4HNO₃ excess O₂ avoids NO loss
Improved absorption with excess O₂ — all NO₂ converted to HNO₃ without releasing NO
INDUS
CONTACT PROCESS — SULPHURIC ACID (H₂SO₄)
R80
S + O₂ → SO₂ burning sulphur / roasting pyrite FeS₂
STEP 1 SO₂ also obtained: 4FeS₂ + 11O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃ + 8SO₂ (roasting of iron pyrite)
REDOX
R81
2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ V₂O₅, 450–550 °C, 1–2 atm ΔH = −197 kJ
STEP 2 — KEY Exothermic + Δn<0 → favour low T, high P. But V₂O₅ active only above 400°C — compromise at 450°C. Yield ~99.7%
INDUS
R82
SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum/fuming H₂SO₄)
STEP 3 SO₃ NOT absorbed directly in water (forms acid mist). Absorbed in conc. H₂SO₄ to give oleum (H₂S₂O₇ = pyrosulphuric acid)
INDUS
R83
H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄
STEP 4 Oleum diluted with water to give conc. H₂SO₄ (98%). Adding SO₃ to water directly would cause explosion
INDUS
DEACON'S PROCESS + CHLORINE PREPARATION
R84
4HCl + O₂ → 2Cl₂ + 2H₂O CuCl₂, 400–500 °C
DEACON'S PROCESS Industrial preparation of Cl₂ from HCl (by-product of organic chemistry). Exothermic, equilibrium favoured at lower T
INDUS
R85
MnO₂ + 4HCl(conc) → MnCl₂ + Cl₂↑ + 2H₂O Δ
LAB PREP Cl₂ MnO₂ oxidises HCl to Cl₂. Mn: +4 → +2. Also: 2KMnO₄ + 16HCl → 2MnCl₂ + 2KCl + 5Cl₂ + 8H₂O
REDOX
THERMITE REACTION & REDUCTION OF METAL OXIDES
R86
Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe ignited by Mg ribbon, T>3000°C
THERMITE Aluminothermy — Al has higher affinity for O₂ than Fe. Temperature high enough to produce molten iron. Used in welding rail tracks
REDOX
R87
Cr₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Cr
Aluminothermy for chromium. Same principle — Al is a better reducing agent than Cr for metal oxide reduction
REDOX
R88
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ blast furnace, ~700–800 °C
IRON EXTRACTION CO reduces Fe₂O₃. Multiple reactions: Fe₂O₃ → Fe₃O₄ → FeO → Fe as CO/CO₂ ratio changes with height in furnace
REDOX
R89
2CuO + C → 2Cu + CO₂ Δ, ~700 °C
Carbon reduces CuO (Ellingham diagram: C line crosses Cu line at ~700°C). Above this temperature C is thermodynamically a better reducing agent
REDOX
R90
ZnO + C → Zn + CO >1000 °C
Coke reduces ZnO. CO is product (not CO₂) at high temperatures — explained by Ellingham diagram (2C+O₂→2CO has negative slope)
REDOX
WATER GAS · PRODUCER GAS · SYNTHESIS GAS
R91
C + H₂O(steam) → CO + H₂ ~1000 °C, endothermic
WATER GAS CO + H₂ mixture. Used as fuel and for H₂ production. Endothermic — needs continuous heat input
REDOX
R92
CO + H₂O(steam) ⇌ CO₂ + H₂ Fe catalyst, 400 °C
WATER-GAS SHIFT Increases H₂ yield. CO₂ removed by scrubbing. Produces pure H₂ for Haber process
INDUS
R93
2C + O₂ + 4N₂(air) → 2CO + 4N₂ producer gas = CO + N₂ mixture
PRODUCER GAS Passing air through red-hot coke. Low calorific value fuel. Used for industrial heating
REDOX
IMPORTANT DISPROPORTIONATION REACTIONS
DISPROPORTIONATION A single species undergoes simultaneous oxidation AND reduction — it acts as both oxidising and reducing agent.
R94
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ MnO₂ catalyst or base
CLASSIC H₂O₂: O is −1 → one goes to 0 (O₂), other to −2 (H₂O). MnO₂, KI, blood, catalase all catalyse this
DISP
R95
Cl₂ + H₂O ⇌ HCl + HOCl Cl: 0 → −1 and +1
CHLORINE WATER Cl₂ disproportionates in water. HOCl (hypochlorous acid) is the bleaching agent — unstable in light (HOCl → HCl + O, nascent oxygen bleaches)
DISP
R96
3Cl₂ + 6NaOH(hot,conc) → NaClO₃ + 5NaCl + 3H₂O Cl: 0 → +5 and −1
HOT vs COLD In hot conc NaOH, Cl disproportionates to +5 and −1. In cold dilute: Cl → +1 (NaOCl) and −1 (NaCl)
DISP
R97
P₄ + 3NaOH + 3H₂O → PH₃↑ + 3NaH₂PO₂ P: 0 → −3 and +1
JEE FREQ White phosphorus disproportionates in NaOH — P (0) → PH₃ (−3) and NaH₂PO₂ sodium hypophosphite (+1). PH₃ is highly toxic
DISP
R98
2NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃ + HNO₂ N: +4 → +5 and +3
NO₂ disproportionates in cold water. In hot water/with excess O₂, all NO₂ → HNO₃
DISP
