1. Drug–target interaction conceptual
Drug — low molecular mass molecule that interacts with a macromolecular target (enzyme/receptor) to produce a biological response.
Target — usually proteins (enzymes or receptors) on cell surface or inside cell.
Enzyme inhibitors: Drug blocks active site (competitive) or allosteric site (non-competitive) → enzyme can't bind substrate.
Receptor agonist: Drug mimics natural messenger, triggers response.
Receptor antagonist: Drug blocks receptor, inhibits natural response (most drugs work this way).
Drug–receptor interaction is mainly through: ionic bonds, H-bonds, van der Waals forces (not covalent — for reversibility).
2. Analgesics — pain relief hot topic
Non-narcotic (non-addictive): Aspirin, Paracetamol, Ibuprofen, Naproxen
— reduce fever, mild/moderate pain. Aspirin also has antiplatelet (blood-thinning) action.
Narcotic (addictive/opiates): Morphine, Codeine, Heroin
— used for severe pain, cause addiction & CNS depression.
Aspirin overdose → peptic ulcer. Paracetamol overdose → liver damage.
3. Tranquilizers & antidepressants
Tranquilizers — reduce anxiety, tension, mental disorders. Act on CNS.
Barbiturates (e.g. Phenobarbitone, Luminal, Seconal) — hypnotic/sedative, habit-forming.
Benzodiazepines (e.g. Valium/Diazepam, Librium/Chlordiazepoxide) — less addictive, widely used for anxiety.
Equanil (Meprobamate) — mild tranquilizer, controls depression & hypertension.
Chlorpromazine, Reserpine — antipsychotic (schizophrenia). Reserpine also lowers BP.
Iproniazid — MAO inhibitor antidepressant (prevents breakdown of serotonin & noradrenaline).
4. Antihistamines & antacids hot topic
Histamine — causes allergic response, stimulates HCl secretion in stomach.
Antihistamines — block histamine receptors, used for allergy/cold:
Brompheniramine (Dimetapp), Terfenadine (Seldane), Diphenhydramine, Cimetidine, Ranitidine.
Cimetidine & Ranitidine → block H₂ receptors in stomach → reduce HCl secretion (used for ulcers).
Antacids — neutralise excess stomach acid. Weak bases only (strong bases would cause alkalosis).
Examples: NaHCO₃, Mg(OH)₂, Al(OH)₃, CaCO₃, Milk of Magnesia.
NaHCO₃ gives CO₂ gas (causes rebound acidity) — Mg(OH)₂ preferred (no gas, no constipation).
5. Antimicrobials & antifertility drugs
Antibiotics — kill or inhibit bacteria. Narrow-spectrum (one type) vs broad-spectrum (many types).
Penicillin — first antibiotic (Fleming, 1929). Narrow-spectrum. Bactericidal (kills bacteria by damaging cell wall).
Ampicillin, Amoxycillin — broad-spectrum semi-synthetic penicillins.
Chloramphenicol — broad-spectrum, used for typhoid, meningitis. Bacteriostatic (stops growth).
Tetracycline, Ofloxacin — broad-spectrum antibiotics.
Sulphonamides (e.g. Sulphapyridine) — earliest antimicrobials. Competitive inhibitors of enzyme making folic acid in bacteria.
Antifertility drugs — synthetic estrogen + progesterone (oral contraceptives). Norethindrone + Ethynylestradiol = most common combination. remember names
6. Artificial sweeteners pure memory
Saccharin — oldest (1879), 550× sweeter than sugar. Excreted unchanged. Used by diabetics.
Aspartame — 100× sweeter. Used in cold drinks & food. Unstable at high temp (not for cooking).
Sucralose — 600× sweeter. Derived from sucrose (trichloro derivative). Stable at cooking temp.
Alitame — 2000× sweeter. Hard to control sweetness level in food.
Cyclamate (Sodium cyclamate) — 30× sweeter. Banned in USA (suspected carcinogen).
7. Soaps, detergents & cleansing action hot topic
Soap — sodium/potassium salt of long-chain fatty acid. Made by saponification of oils/fats with NaOH.
Soap molecule has: hydrophilic head (–COO⁻Na⁺, ionic) + hydrophobic tail (long hydrocarbon chain).
Micelle formation — tails point inward (into oil/grease), heads point outward (into water). Grease is trapped inside micelle and washed away.
Soaps don't work in hard water — Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ ions form insoluble scum: 2RCOONa + CaCl₂ → (RCOO)₂Ca↓ + 2NaCl
Detergents — sodium salts of sulphonic acids or sulphate esters. Work in hard water (Ca/Mg salts are soluble).
Anionic detergent (most common) — e.g. Sodium lauryl sulphate. Hydrophilic: –OSO₃⁻Na⁺
Cationic detergent — quaternary ammonium salts. Germicidal + fabric softener. E.g. cetyltrimethyl ammonium bromide.
Non-ionic detergent — e.g. liquid dishwash soaps. No ionic charge, forms micelles.
Detergents with branched hydrocarbon chains → non-biodegradable (cause foam in rivers). Straight chain → biodegradable.
